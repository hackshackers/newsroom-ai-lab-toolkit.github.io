# Overview

Now we're moving into the **define** stage of Design Thinking. The first portion of this is refining the information you gathered from the empathize stage into structured "problem statements", in the format:

<p className="problem-statement-formula">
  <span className="highlight-user">[User]</span> <em>needs</em> <span className="highlight-need">[need]</span> <em>so that</em> <span className="highlight-goal">[goal]</span>.
</p>

- **<span className="highlight-user">User</span>**: *Who* is affected? (Reporter, editor, another specific role, a specific desk or team, the whole newsroom?)
- **<span className="highlight-need">Need</span>**: *What* do they need to accomplish, avoid, or understand?
- **<span className="highlight-goal">Goal</span>**: *Why* does it matter? What's the bigger outcome or value?

## Why create structured Problem Statements?

Before we start exploring ideas or evaluating possible tools, we need a clear, shared understanding of **what problem we're actually trying to solve** and **for whom**. A structured problem statement gives us that clarity.

In day-to-day newsroom work, **pain points and opportunities often surface as loose observations** ("the CMS feels slow", "pulling data takes forever", "I wish we could do X", etc). These are valuable signals, but they are not actually actionable. Turning them into a structured statement is how we move from *intuition* to *definition*.

## Problem Statements help us:

### 1. Separate the problem from the solution

When frustrations or ideas are first shared, they often come bundled with assumptions about how to fix them.

A structured format helps us **pause long enough to understand what the user actually needs *before* we consider technical options**. This protects us from prematurely choosing tools that don't solve the underlying issue.

### 2. Reveal gaps in our understanding

When you try to write a problem statement and something feels vague or missing, that's a signal that you need to go back and clarify with the people directly impacted by the situation.

Ambiguity is often a sign that you do not yet understand the user's experience well enough. Writing structured problem statements helps make those gaps visible.

### 3. Make the need specific and observable

A phrase like "reporters need an easier workflow" is too broad to design around. But a clear statement like:

> <span className="highlight-user">Metro reporters</span> need <span className="highlight-need">to quickly find usable quotes from long city council meetings</span> so that <span className="highlight-goal">they can turn around accurate stories on deadline</span>

reveals who is affected, where the friction occurs, and why it matters. That level of precision makes the next stages of design thinking far more targeted and effective.

### 4. Build alignment across roles

Editors, reporters, researchers, product, audience, and business teams often use different shorthand to describe the same issue. Problem statements **give everyone a common language** for discussing the user, the need, and the goal. When the group eventually evaluates ideas, they're all anchored to the same user need and goal.

### 5. Connect individual pain points to broader organizational goals and priorities

Writing the statement in a "so that…" format pushes us to explain why the need matters in practice. A strong goal makes the downstream consequence visible: meeting deadline, avoiding missed context, improving accuracy, covering underheard communities more consistently, or reducing duplication. This helps teams judge which problems are meaningful and worth pursuing.

### 6. Enable better brainstorming — and better solutions

Clear problem definitions unlock more creative, targeted ideation. Without them, groups tend to generate solutions that are either too generic, too technical, or disconnected from the actual need. Structured problem statements support better ideation and make it easier to compare ideas later.

---

**Structured problem statements help ensure we're solving the right problem, in the right way, for the right people.** They are the bridge between raw observations in the Empathize stage and informed, testable ideas in the Ideate and Prototype stages.
