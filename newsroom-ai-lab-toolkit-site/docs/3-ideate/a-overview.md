# ♻️ Iterative Development

With a clear problem brief in hand, you are ready to start building toward a solution. The Iterate stage is where you move from definition to action - generating ideas, prototyping quickly, and testing with real users.

Each cycle brings you closer to something that actually works.

Earlier stages of the Design Thinking process focus on observing, listening, defining the problem clearly, and clarifying what success should look like before jumping to the solution itself.

### 👷Now it’s time to start building\!

### …but *what* are we building?

### … we don’t know exactly, yet.… and that’s intentional.

When you try to define the entire solution upfront, you have to make a lot of assumptions about most effective approaches \- **early guesses can harden into plans.** 

If we pretend we have solution certainty too early, we’ll most likely end up very **carefully building the wrong thing.**

### You may build something ✨beautiful✨ that does not actually serve the people it was meant to help.

## Iterative development says:

We know enough to take a thoughtful next step.  
We do not know enough to assume the whole plan is right.  
We will test, learn, adjust, and keep moving towards the solution.

A useful iteration is **a focused cycle that helps the team learn something important**.

Rapid, grounded cycles add up to a solution that is much more likely to actually solve the core problem.

## Contrasts with the ✨\#classic ✨ approach to project management 

### …AKA The Waterfall method which involves non-iterative, incremental development

- Begins with a **fully comprehensive solution specification**  
- A sequential development process that flows like a waterfall through all phases of a project with **each phase completely wrapping up before the next phase begins**  
- **Stakeholders are only involved at the start and the end**

### 🌉 Waterfall is essential when the blueprint is knowable (think: a bridge)

### … but gets in the way when the solution has to be uncovered

### The Waterfall approach is tricky when building something totally new 

#### **🔮“mythical certainty"**

- **Requires a fully comprehensive specification** at the start of development  
- The process of writing the spec is time-consuming and necessitates making **assumptions about things that are truthfully unknowable at the start**  
- **High precision, low accuracy**  
- Relies on users / stakeholders having **a very clear idea of what they need** and the ability to **articulate it in precise detail**

#### 💸**Changes can be costly** since work (incremental steps) and timelines are planned in detail, in advance

- Since the each stage is dependent on the outcome of the previous, **even a small change in the project scope can cause big challenges**  
- If requirements aren’t crystal clear or available at the start, there can be significant complications later on

#### ⏳**No usable tool/solution is produced until all work is complete**

#### **🗣️No opportunity for continuous user feedback**

#### ❌🤸**Low flexibility** in the process

- Projects face diﬀiculty when there are high-level changes in requirements or new ideas

## …there’s a better way\! 

# ♻️ Iterative Method 

### Create the Mona Lisa, but iteratively this time:
 
*Source: [The Agile Broadcast \- YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ITOWKUjr8VA)*

### Iterative increments:

1. **“Working" prototype** **→ stakeholder feedback & evaluation of value**  
2. **Make some changes** to hand positioning and background **→ stakeholder feedback & evaluation of value**  
3. **Add** some basic colors **→ stakeholder feedback & evaluation of value**  
4. **Alter** colors and **add** some more detail **→ deliver to stakeholders** (and/or continue iterating based on feedback)

## The **concept and the solution eventually converge**

- You progress toward your desired result each time you iterate

You may not (and likely don’t\!) have the right answer initially…

- you can **start with an informed guess**,  
- **review** the initial solution,  
- **refine** the guess,  
- **review** the revised solution, and  
- **♻️repeat** until you get to the answer

### What makes iterative, incremental development tricky?

- **Requires engaged stakeholders** who are willing and able to be **involved throughout the development process**  
- **Funders** might require a detailed, long term plan in order to get grant $

### If we’re continuously iterating, **how can we know when the project will be "done"**?

- Iterative development doesn’t mean we’ll be done when we’re done  
- We can still forecast timeline, but we do in shorter increments (ie sprints)  
- This may be a big adjustment for some team members and stakeholders.  
  - Ideally, in re-framing the builder \<\> stakeholder relationship from transactional to collaborative so that stakeholders are engaged in the development and have a nuanced understanding of progress via continuous delivery of value

# Effective iteration requires short, clear cycles of work 

The goal of each cycle is to r**educe ambiguity and make clear, measurable progress** towards the ultimate solution. 

This often looks like defining a concise hypothesis or evaluating an approach to solving one piece of the problem. 

Cycles are short \- weeks not months. 

# 💡A couple tips / suggestions

### “Fail" forward ▶️

- Purposefully and deliberately **use"failure“ to find success** \- perfect is the enemy of the good enough  
- Over time failing forward helps us get better at mitigating risk  
  -  … BUT there will always need to be some **tolerance for risk**  
  - **Re-evaluate after each iteration** (eg 2-week sprint)  
    - This keeps risks from growing like they might if the only evaluation happened at the end of the project

### ⭐Keep focused on the north star, but don’t think too far down the road

- A key goal of iterative planning is to **avoid over planning** \- since this is often based on 🔮“mythical certainty"  
- Each time you come to a **decision point**, consider:  
  - ‘what is the best option given what we know at this point, which will minimally limit future options’  
  - This framing can help ensure iterations are thoughtful and well-engineered  
  - iterative \!= scrappy  
- Always **consider what decisions must be made now, vs those that can wait**  
- Eg:  
  - Choosing a newsletter’s title text design doesn’t need to block selecting the stories that will be included  
- BUT, always **consider how each choice made might impact possibilities farther down the line**  
  - AKA \- don’t build yourself into a corner by making hasty decisions

### 🧘Don’t get too attached

- Sometimes you will scrap something you spent time on \- that’s ok❣️  
- Use what you learned in the process to come up with a better solution.  
  - See"Fail" forward ▶️👆

### ☑️Debate, decide, commit (+ document)

- Be thoughtful, but don’t dwell on having perfect data before making a decision  
- Capture why each decision is made and what information you used to come to the conclusion  
  - This is in service of your future selves and teammates  
  - Eg: *We looked at both X and Y as database options \- here are the pro/cons we found for each… We went with Y for A, B, C reasons.*  
- Worst case, you commit to the decision for two weeks of work, then get more information which allows you to pivot to a better, more informed option

### 💞Engage stakeholders

- **The best stakeholders are the ones that are the most continuously invested**  
  - Feedback on each iteration is a must in order to generate the most valuable final product  
- Consider scheduling a bi-weekly meeting that aligns with sprint cadence, where you give demo new value created, receive feedback, and ask/answer questions  
- Consider ways to structure cross-team meetings to ensure you are **showing progress *and* getting meaningful feedback from users**.

### 🏗️Break down work as much as possible

- The smaller the individual tasks, the better the effort estimation, the greater the chance of success

### 🎉Celebrate regularly

- Thoughtful and responsible sprint planning should lead to meaningful progress and a sense of accomplishment (at least) every two weeks \- celebrate that\!  
- Even better if you can do this with your stakeholder partners.
