# ♻️ Iterative Development

With a clear problem brief in hand, you are ready to start building toward a solution. The Iterate stage is where you move from definition to action - generating ideas, prototyping quickly, and testing with real users.

Each cycle brings you closer to something that actually works.

Earlier stages of the Design Thinking process focus on observing, listening, defining the problem clearly, and clarifying what success should look like before jumping to the solution itself.

## 👷Now it’s time to start building\!

…but *what* are we building?

… we don’t know exactly, yet.… and that’s intentional.

When you try to define the entire solution upfront, you have to make a lot of assumptions about most effective approaches \- **early guesses can harden into plans.** 

If we pretend we have solution certainty too early, we’ll most likely end up very **carefully building the wrong thing.**

#### You may build something ✨beautiful✨ that does not actually serve the people it was meant to help.

## Iterative development says:

1. We know enough to take a thoughtful next step.  
2. We do not know enough to assume the whole plan is right.  
3. We will test, learn, adjust, and keep moving towards the solution.

A useful iteration is **a focused cycle that helps the team learn something important**.

Rapid, grounded cycles add up to a solution that is much more likely to actually solve the core problem.

## Contrasts with the ✨\#classic ✨ approach to project management: Waterfall 

#### The Waterfall method involves non-iterative, incremental development which:

- Begins with a **fully comprehensive solution specification**  
- Relies on a pre-defined, sequential development process that flows like a waterfall through all phases of a project with **each phase completely wrapping up before the next phase begins**  
- **Stakeholders are only involved at the start and the end**

## The **concept and the solution eventually converge** - you progress toward your desired result each time you iterate

You may not (and likely don’t\!) have the right answer initially, but you can:

- **start with an informed guess**,  
- **review** the initial solution,  
- **refine** the guess,  
- **review** the revised solution, and  
- **♻️repeat** until you get to the answer

### What makes iterative, incremental development tricky?

- **Requires engaged stakeholders** who are willing and able to be **involved throughout the development process**  
- **Funders** might require a detailed, long term plan in order to get grant $

### If we’re continuously iterating, how can we know when the project will be "done"?
- Iterative development doesn’t mean we’ll be done when we’re done  
- We can still forecast timeline, but we do in shorter increments (ie sprints)  
- This may be a big adjustment for some team members and stakeholders.  
  - You will **re-frame the builder \<\> stakeholder relationship from transactional to collaborative**. Their engagement in the development process will give them a nuanced understanding of progress via continuous delivery of value.

# Effective iteration requires short, clear cycles of work 

The goal of each cycle is to r**educe ambiguity and make clear, measurable progress** towards the ultimate solution. 

This often looks like defining a concise hypothesis or evaluating an approach to solving one piece of the problem. 

Cycles are short \- weeks not months.
